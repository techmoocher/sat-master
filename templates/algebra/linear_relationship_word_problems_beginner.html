Linear Relationship Word Problems

Understanding and applying linear relationships in real-world contexts

Linear Relationships in Context

Linear relationship problems involve identifying and using linear patterns between two variables. These questions often ask you to find rates, interpret slopes and y-intercepts, and make predictions based on linear models.

Key Concepts to Remember
  • Slope represents rate of change (e.g., cost per unit, speed)
  • Y-intercept often represents an initial value or fixed cost
  • Linear equation format: y = mx + b
  • Direct proportions: y = kx (where k is the constant of proportionality)

Rate and Unit Rate Problems

Example: A car travels 240 miles using 8 gallons of gas. At this rate, how many gallons of gas would the car use to travel 360 miles?

Step 1: Find the unit rate (miles per gallon).

240 miles ÷ 8 gallons = 30 miles per gallon

Step 2: Use the rate to find the answer.

360 miles ÷ 30 miles per gallon = 12 gallons

Alternative method: Set up a proportion.

8 gallons / 240 miles = x gallons / 360 miles

8/240 = x/360

8 × 360 = 240 × x

2,880 = 240x

x = 12 gallons

Cost and Revenue Models

Example: A small business has fixed costs of $1,200 per month and variable costs of $15 per unit produced. The business sells each unit for $40.

a) Write an equation for the total monthly cost (C) in terms of the number of units (n) produced.

b) Write an equation for the total monthly revenue (R) in terms of the number of units (n) sold.

c) How many units must be sold for the business to break even (Revenue = Cost)?

a) Cost equation:

Total cost = Fixed costs + Variable costs

C = 1,200 + 15n

b) Revenue equation:

Revenue = Price per unit × Number of units

R = 40n

c) Break-even point:

Revenue = Cost

40n = 1,200 + 15n

40n - 15n = 1,200

25n = 1,200

n = 48 units

Distance-Time-Rate Problems

Example: Jesse rides his bike at a constant speed of 12 miles per hour. Write a linear equation that relates the distance (d) traveled to the time (t) in hours. Then calculate how long it will take Jesse to travel 42 miles.

Step 1: Write the linear equation.

d = 12t

Where d is distance in miles and t is time in hours

Step 2: Solve for the time to travel 42 miles.

42 = 12t

t = 42 ÷ 12 = 3.5 hours

3.5 hours = 3 hours and 30 minutes

Linear Growth and Depreciation

Example: A new car valued at $25,000 depreciates by $3,000 per year. Write a linear equation for the car's value (V) after t years. How much will the car be worth after 4 years?

Step 1: Identify the initial value and rate of change.

Initial value = $25,000

Rate of change = -$3,000 per year (negative because the value decreases)

Step 2: Write the linear equation in slope-intercept form.

V = -3,000t + 25,000

Step 3: Calculate the value after 4 years.

V = -3,000(4) + 25,000

V = -12,000 + 25,000

V = $13,000

Practice Questions

1. A taxi charges $2.50 for the first mile and $1.75 for each additional mile. Write a linear equation for the total fare (F) in terms of the distance (d) in miles (where d ≥ 1). Then find the fare for a 7-mile trip.

For d ≥ 1: F = 2.50 + 1.75(d - 1) F = 2.50 + 1.75d - 1.75 F = 0.75 + 1.75d For a 7-mile trip: F = 0.75 + 1.75(7) F = 0.75 + 12.25 F = $13.00

2. A water tank initially has 500 gallons of water. Water is being drained at a constant rate of 25 gallons per minute.

a) Write an equation for the amount of water (W) in the tank after t minutes.

b) How long will it take for the tank to be half full?

a) Initial amount minus what's drained: W = 500 - 25t b) To find when the tank is half full (250 gallons): 250 = 500 - 25t -250 = -25t t = 10 minutes

3. A company charges a monthly subscription fee of $10 plus $5 per hour of service used. If a customer's bill for one month is $45, how many hours of service did they use?

Let h = hours of service Total bill = Monthly fee + Hourly fee × Hours 45 = 10 + 5h 35 = 5h h = 7 hours

SAT Tips for Linear Relationship Problems

  • Identify the components of the linear model (slope and y-intercept) and understand what they represent in the given context.
  • Remember that the slope represents the rate of change (for each unit increase in x, y increases by the slope value).
  • Pay attention to units and ensure they're consistent throughout your solution.
  • When interpreting models, consider whether the relationship makes sense over the entire domain or only for a restricted range of values.
  • Watch out for "hidden" fixed costs or starting values that might not be explicitly stated but can be inferred from the problem.
