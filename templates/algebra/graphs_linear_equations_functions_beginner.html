Graphs of Linear Equations and Functions

Understanding the visual representation of linear relationships

Understanding Linear Graphs

Graphs of linear equations are straight lines that represent the set of all solutions to the equation. Being able to interpret these graphs is essential for the SAT Math section, as many problems involve analyzing, comparing, and creating linear models.

Key Concepts
  • Slope-intercept form: y = mx + b, where m is the slope and b is the y-intercept
  • Point-slope form: y - y₁ = m(x - x₁), where m is the slope and (x₁, y₁) is a point on the line
  • Standard form: Ax + By = C, where A, B, and C are constants
  • Horizontal lines have equation y = k (slope = 0)
  • Vertical lines have equation x = h (undefined slope)

Slope and Y-Intercept

Slope (m): The slope measures the steepness of a line. It is the ratio of the vertical change to the horizontal change between any two points on the line.

Slope formula: m = (y₂ - y₁) / (x₂ - x₁)

Slope interpretation:

  • Positive slope: Line rises from left to right
  • Negative slope: Line falls from left to right
  • Zero slope: Horizontal line
  • Undefined slope: Vertical line

Y-intercept (b): The y-intercept is the y-coordinate of the point where the line crosses the y-axis (when x = 0).

Positive slope: Line rises from left to right

Negative slope: Line falls from left to right

Graphing Linear Equations

Example: Graph the line y = 2x - 3

Method 1: Using the y-intercept and slope

Step 1: Identify the y-intercept (b = -3).

Plot the point (0, -3).

Step 2: Use the slope to find another point.

Slope = 2 = rise/run = 2/1

From (0, -3), move up 2 units and right 1 unit to get point (1, -1).

Step 3: Draw a line through these points.

Method 2: Creating a table of values

Step 1: Choose several x-values.

Step 2: Calculate the corresponding y-values using the equation y = 2x - 3.

x y = 2x - 3 Point (x, y)
-1 y = 2(-1) - 3 = -5 (-1, -5)
0 y = 2(0) - 3 = -3 (0, -3)
1 y = 2(1) - 3 = -1 (1, -1)
2 y = 2(2) - 3 = 1 (2, 1)

Step 3: Plot the points and draw a line through them.

Finding the Equation of a Line

Example 1: Find the equation of a line with slope m = 3 passing through the point (2, 5).

Step 1: Use the point-slope form.

y - y₁ = m(x - x₁)

y - 5 = 3(x - 2)

Step 2: Simplify to slope-intercept form.

y - 5 = 3x - 6

y = 3x - 6 + 5

y = 3x - 1

Example 2: Find the equation of a line passing through the points (1, 3) and (4, 9).

Step 1: Calculate the slope.

m = (y₂ - y₁) / (x₂ - x₁) = (9 - 3) / (4 - 1) = 6 / 3 = 2

Step 2: Use point-slope form with one of the points.

y - 3 = 2(x - 1)

Step 3: Convert to slope-intercept form.

y - 3 = 2x - 2

y = 2x - 2 + 3

y = 2x + 1

Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

Parallel Lines:

  • Have the same slope
  • If line 1 has slope m₁, and line 2 has slope m₂, then they are parallel if m₁ = m₂

Example: The line y = 2x + 5 is parallel to y = 2x - 3 because both have slope m = 2.

Perpendicular Lines:

  • Have slopes that are negative reciprocals of each other
  • If line 1 has slope m₁, and line 2 has slope m₂, then they are perpendicular if m₁ × m₂ = -1

Example: The line y = 2x + 1 is perpendicular to y = -1/2 x + 4 because 2 × (-1/2) = -1.

Practice Questions

1. What is the slope of a line that passes through the points (2, 5) and (6, 13)?

m = (y₂ - y₁) / (x₂ - x₁) m = (13 - 5) / (6 - 2) m = 8 / 4 m = 2 The slope is 2.

2. Find the equation of a line in slope-intercept form that passes through the point (3, -2) and has a slope of -4.

Using the point-slope form: y - y₁ = m(x - x₁) y - (-2) = -4(x - 3) y + 2 = -4x + 12 y = -4x + 10

3. Line l is given by the equation 3x - 6y = 12. Which of the following lines is perpendicular to line l?

A) y = 2x + 1

B) y = 1/2 x + 3

C) y = -1/2 x + 2

D) y = -2x - 4

First, rewrite 3x - 6y = 12 in slope-intercept form: -6y = -3x + 12 y = (1/2)x - 2 The slope is 1/2. For a perpendicular line, the slope must be the negative reciprocal: -2 Only option D has a slope of -2, so the answer is D) y = -2x - 4

SAT Tips for Linear Graphs

  • Be comfortable converting between different forms of linear equations (slope-intercept, point-slope, standard).
  • Remember that the x-intercept is the point where the line crosses the x-axis (y = 0).
  • When graphing, check your work by substituting a point back into the original equation.
  • For word problems, pay attention to the context - the slope often represents a rate (e.g., dollars per hour, miles per gallon).
  • On the SAT, sometimes the most efficient approach is to pick values and test them in the given equations rather than solving algebraically.
